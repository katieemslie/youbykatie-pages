A learned skill

Creative briefing is
a lot like translating
a language

When you first learn a new language, you translate word-for-word. It's clunky. Things get lost. But as you become fluent, you stop translating individual words and start translating meaning. You learn which words to use in each language to convey the same idea.

Creative briefing works the same way. It's the translation of ideas. And just like learning a language, you need to understand what to include and how to express it to give your recipient the best chance at understanding the meaning behind your vision.

What business owners get wrong

Assuming that because someone is "creative," they'll just get it. That they'll be able to understand your idea straight away without context, direction, or parameters. Creativity needs constraints to work within. Without them, your designer is guessing.

What designers get wrong

Assuming they know what the business owner means without clarifying or confirming. Running with an interpretation instead of asking the right questions. A good brief removes the need for mind-reading on both sides.

Two sides, one solution

A brief that works
for everyone

Most briefs fail because one side is guessing. A good brief removes the guesswork entirely.

Business owners assume

That because the professional is "creative," they'll just understand the vision straight away. That showing a Pinterest board is enough. That the details will sort themselves out as the project progresses.

Designers assume

That they know what the client means without clarifying. That their interpretation is correct. That asking too many questions will make them look inexperienced.

The solution

A brief that outlines clear objectives, visual guidelines and guardrails, gives the needed context, and allows space for explanation, brand personality expression, and articulation of your inspiration. One document. Both sides aligned.

The secret of the briefing process

The brief isn't actually
for your designer

Well, okay, it is. But it's also for you.

By the time you've worked through the briefing process, you'll have refined and analysed your goals and objectives. You'll have gained clarity on what you actually want. Sometimes you'll discover you weren't as clear on the direction as you thought, and that's incredibly valuable to realise before the project starts rather than halfway through revisions.

A brief is a thinking tool disguised as a communication tool. Use it as both and your creative projects will be completely different.

This clarity makes feedback and revision straightforward. Instead of "something feels off," you come back to the brief as your reference point and say exactly what isn't aligned. That's a different conversation. And it leads to different outcomes.
