How to Brief Like a Pro

The translation
problem

You know what you want. You can picture it perfectly. But the moment you try to communicate that vision to a designer or creative professional, something gets lost in translation.

That gap between what you can see in your head and what you can put into words? That's where projects go sideways, budgets blow out, and everyone ends up frustrated.

I've been on both sides of this for 15 years. I've been the creative director briefing teams, and I've been the designer receiving a brief that says "make it feel premium" with no other context. So I want to be a bridge between those two worlds.

This guide walks you through 8 essential principles for briefing a creative professional. Not just what to include, but why each piece of information matters and how it shapes the quality of work you receive.

Good design isn't just about making things look beautiful. It's about solving a communication problem.

A clear brief gives the context, direction, and boundaries needed to make strategic creative decisions. It exists to make the process easier for both sides.

Use this guide as a thinking tool. Not a checklist to stress over.

How to Brief Like a Pro
01

Describe the destination, not the route

The most common briefing mistake is being either too vague or too prescriptive. "Make it blue with the logo on the left" tells your designer what to build, not what it needs to achieve.

Instead, try: "We need a campaign banner that positions us as the premium option in our market. Our audience is women 35-50 who value quality over price."

The first version makes your designer a pair of hands. The second makes them a strategic partner. The second one will always produce better work, because now they're solving a problem, not following instructions.

Tell your designer where you need to end up. Let them figure out the best way to get there. That's what you're paying them for.
02

Always share your budget

Creativity is not a finite thing. We're not plumbers. It's not like it generally takes X amount of time to fix a toilet.

The creative problem we're solving requires different amounts of energy and input depending on the scope. We need to know what your budget is so we know the parameters of the project, because we could keep creating forever.

You can be conservative with your budget. But please give us something to work with. It makes it so much easier to know what we're able to deliver and ensures the scope matches your investment.

03

Include brand context, even when they "already know"

People skip this all the time. They think their designer already knows the brand. And maybe they do.

But when you write out your brand positioning, your competitors, and how you want people to feel when they interact with your brand, you're forcing yourself to articulate things you usually keep in your head. Sometimes writing it down reveals you're not as clear on it as you thought. That's incredibly valuable to discover before a project starts, not halfway through revisions.

Also: we may not know your competitive landscape. We might design something in orange that happens to be the signature colour of your direct competitor. Give us enough context to make you visually distinct.

Even with a designer who knows your brand well, completing this section means everyone is literally on the same page. Assumptions are where projects get expensive.
04

Describe what you like about your inspiration, not just show it

Sending your designer a Pinterest board or a few screenshots is a great start. But the images aren't the most important part. The description underneath is.

If you paste in an image and say "I love the typography here," your designer knows: okay, we're not focusing on colour in this reference, they're drawn to the type treatment. That's a specific, actionable direction. An image without a description is a guessing game. An image with a description is a strategic direction.

05

Show what you don't want (anti-inspiration)

This is what I wish every brief I received included.

When you tell a designer "I like minimalist design," that could mean a hundred different things. But when you show three examples and say "not this, not this, and definitely not this," you've just eliminated entire directions they might have gone down. You've potentially saved a full round of revisions before the project has even started.

Tell your designer why you don't like something, not just that you don't. "I don't like this because it feels too corporate" gives so much more to work with than just crossing something out.

Anti-inspiration isn't negative. It's one of the most constructive things you can give a creative professional. It's clarity about where the edges are.
06

Use personality, not jargon

You don't need to speak colour theory or know your brand archetype. You just need to communicate the feeling.

Exercises like "What would your brand wear to a party?" sound simple, but the information they give your designer is incredibly useful. A brand that shows up in heels and a blazer is a completely different design direction than one that arrives in a flowy dress and sneakers. That single image tells your designer about tone, formality, energy, and aesthetic all at once.

Similarly, choosing 3-5 descriptive words for your brand's personality (warm, bold, whimsical, refined, spirited) gives specific parameters. These words directly inform font choices, layout decisions, imagery style, and colour palette.

07

Be specific about deliverables and technical specs

This is the least glamorous part of any brief and it will save you the most money.

Not just "social media graphics" but which platforms, what sizes, how many variations. Not just "a logo" but where it needs to work: website header, business card, social media avatar, embroidered on a hat. When your designer knows the full scope from day one, they design systems that work across everything rather than individual pieces that don't translate.

Technical specs (file formats, dimensions, colour modes) and any legal or industry requirements also belong here. Every detail you provide upfront is one fewer email back and forth during the project.

08

Remember: the brief is actually for you

Here's what nobody tells you about briefing. The brief isn't really for your designer. I mean, it is. They need the information. But the real value is what happens to your own thinking when you work through it.

By the time you've completed a thorough brief, you've thought about what you want, what you don't want, who it's for, what it needs to achieve, and what the practical requirements are. That clarity shows up everywhere: in how you give feedback, how you make decisions during revisions, how you evaluate the work.

Instead of "something feels off," you can say "this doesn't align with the personality direction we agreed on." That's a completely different conversation. And it leads to completely different outcomes.

A brief is a thinking tool disguised as a communication tool. Use it as both and your creative projects will be completely different.

Quick
reference

Before you send your next brief, run through this checklist. You don't need every item for every project, but reading through each one will help you spot gaps.

Project Objective

What does this project need to achieve? Describe the destination, not the route.

Intended Audience

Who is this for? The more specific, the better the design decisions.

Timeline + Budget

When do you need it? What's the investment? Both shape the scope.

Brand Context

Positioning, competitors, existing guidelines. Even if they "already know."

Visual Inspiration

Images with descriptions of what you like about each one.

Anti-Inspiration

What you don't want and why. Your most powerful briefing tool.

Brand Personality

Colour direction, vibe words, what your brand would wear.

Deliverables + Specs

Exactly what's needed, in what formats, at what sizes.
